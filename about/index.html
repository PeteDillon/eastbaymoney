About Pete

From the natural world
to the human one

"While physics and chemistry explain much of the physical world, economics, finance and financial accounting explain much of the human world."

I grew up in Oregon and gravitated toward science early — graduating with a degree in chemistry from Macalester College in Minnesota, then spending a year as a chemist at 3M before coming to California to earn a graduate degree in atmospheric chemistry from UC Berkeley. Chemistry, as I saw it then, was a beautiful thing: rigorous, internally consistent, and highly explanatory. A set of tools for understanding why the world works the way it does.

But during graduate school, connections with Haas business school students through the Berkeley Energy and Resources Collaborative started to expand what I thought my interests could be. I went into environmental consulting at ERM — air-quality permitting, compliance for oil and gas clients, mining, aluminum smelting — and eventually found myself embedded at Chevron's headquarters in San Ramon, where I owned quarterly internal reporting of energy-intensity index metrics that rolled up to exec- and board-level reporting. My client sat within corporate accounting. I admired the rigor and operational discipline I saw there.

"I began to see that while physics and chemistry explain much of the physical world, economics, finance and financial accounting explain much of the human world."

Around the same time, I read Titan — Ron Chernow's biography of John D. Rockefeller, who had trained as an accountant. It was the first time I'd ever seriously considered accounting as a discipline. The story that stuck with me: Rockefeller noticed a technician using 40 drops of solder to seal each oil can. He tried 38 — too many leaks. At 39, the cans held. He had lowered costs by 2.5 percent by looking closely at something everyone else had stopped seeing. That kind of thinking appealed to me.

I went back to Berkeley — this time for an MBA at Haas, focusing on finance and strategy — and then pursued the UC Berkeley Extension Certificate in Accounting to close the gaps. A mentor, Richard Long, a seasoned Silicon Valley CFO and former accountant, opened my eyes to the crucial and powerful role that accounting plays in building and operating great organizations.

What followed was a career at the intersection of finance, tax, and operations: FP&A at Color Health, finance director at KETOS, strategic finance at Luminary Cloud, and currently senior finance manager at the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, where I lead FP&A partnership to the Immunization team within the Global Development division. Through all of it, the through-line has been the same impulse that drew me to chemistry: a deep desire to understand how things work — and to use that understanding to help people and organizations do better.

I'm working toward my CPA license and spending this spring in the East Bay doing something a little different — getting out into the community, listening, and learning. If something's on your mind, I'd genuinely love to hear it.
