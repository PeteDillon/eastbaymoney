Working with Pete
How does the first conversation work?

You leave your info on the Let's Talk page and I'll follow up within a day or two to set up a call or meeting. The first conversation is free — no agenda, no pitch, just a chance to talk through what's on your mind and see if working together makes sense.

If it does, we'll agree on scope and fees before anything starts. If it doesn't — if your situation is better served by someone else — I'll tell you that, and I'll try to point you in the right direction.

What does fee-only mean?

It means I'm compensated only by the fees you pay me directly — not by commissions, referral fees, or any percentage of assets. I don't sell financial products and I have no financial relationship with any product provider.

This matters because most financial advisors make money when you buy something from them. Fee-only advisors don't, which means our incentives are actually aligned: I only benefit when you get good advice.

What does it mean to work with someone who is fiduciary?

A fiduciary is legally and ethically obligated to act in your best interest — not just to recommend something "suitable" for you, which is a much weaker standard. I operate as a fiduciary: your interests come first, full stop.

Many financial advisors and brokers are held only to a "suitability" standard, which means they can recommend products that are good enough for you even if better options exist — particularly if the better option pays them less commission. Fiduciary advisors don't have that conflict.

How do you charge?

It depends on the scope. For a focused question or a single decision — tax strategy for a liquidity event, whether to buy or rent, how to structure equity comp — I typically charge a flat project fee we agree on upfront.

For ongoing work — fractional finance leadership, quarterly planning, retainer relationships — we'd agree on a monthly or annual arrangement. I'll always tell you what something will cost before we start.

Do you work with people outside the East Bay?

The East Bay is my community and where I'm most focused, but geography isn't really a constraint for most of what I do. If you're somewhere else in the Bay Area — or elsewhere entirely — reach out and we can figure it out.

Business & Tax Questions
When should a business owner think about entity structure?

Earlier than most people do. The structure you choose — sole proprietorship, LLC, S-Corp, C-Corp — has real tax and legal consequences that are much easier to address before they become problems. Common triggers to revisit it: your business is consistently profitable, you're bringing on partners or investors, you're thinking about equity compensation, or you're planning an eventual exit.

If you're not sure whether your current structure still makes sense, that's a good question for a first conversation.

What is QSBS and why does it matter?

Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS) is a provision in the tax code that allows founders and early investors in qualifying C-corporations to exclude up to $10 million in capital gains — federal, and potentially state — when they sell. It's one of the most valuable tax benefits available to startup founders, and one of the most underused.

The catch: the rules are specific and the planning needs to happen well before a sale. If you're a founder or early employee at a startup, this is worth understanding sooner rather than later.

What's the difference between tax planning and tax preparation?

Tax preparation is the compliance work of filing your return — reporting what happened last year. Tax planning is the strategy work of deciding what to do this year, and next year, to minimize what you owe over time.

Most CPAs and tax preparers focus on preparation. Planning — decisions about timing, structure, accounts, and transactions — often happens in a different conversation, and often not at all. That's the gap I work in.

Personal Finance
I have equity compensation (RSUs, options) and don't fully understand it. Can you help?

Yes, and you're not alone. RSUs, ISOs, NSOs, and ESPPs each have different tax treatment, vesting mechanics, and planning considerations. The stakes are high — especially around timing of exercises and sales — and the documentation is usually written in a way that doesn't make the trade-offs obvious.

I can help you understand what you have, model the tax outcomes of different scenarios, and think through a strategy that fits your broader financial picture.

Should I buy or rent in the East Bay right now?

There's no universal answer — it depends on your income, savings, timeline, and what you value. What I can do is help you run the actual numbers for your specific situation: what you'd pay to own vs. rent over a given time horizon, how your tax situation changes, and what the trade-offs look like given where you are financially.

Housing is one of the most emotionally loaded financial decisions people make. Having a clear-eyed view of the math helps.

I'm behind on retirement savings. Is it too late?

Almost certainly not. But the answer depends on your age, income, existing savings, Social Security situation, and what kind of retirement you're imagining. There are often more levers available than people realize — particularly for business owners and self-employed people, who have access to retirement vehicles with much higher contribution limits than typical employees.

This is a great topic for a first conversation.
